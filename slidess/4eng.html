Newtonian Physics

Laws of movement

  1. Every object will remain at rest or in uniform motion in a straight line unless compelled to change its state by the action of an external force.
    (example: circular movement).
  2. For an externally applied force, the change in velocity depends on the mass of the object
    F = ma2. (Ex. Free fall : initial height - ( g × time 2 ÷ 2))
  3. For every action (force) in nature there is an equal and opposite reaction (Ex: jumping on solid ground vs jumping on mud)

Law of universal gravitation

Gravitational force = G * (M1 * M2) / D2

Gravitational force =
Gravitational constant × mass1 × mass2 ÷ distancia2

Gravitational force =
Gravitational constant (6.67E-11 m3 s-2 kg-1) × mass1 (objeto uno) × mass2 (objeto dos) ÷ distance between the objects2

One of the main virtues of this view is its scope. The same laws that govern the movement of the planets, govern the movment of the simple board marker. There are no special rules, some for the sublunar, some for the supralunar.

There is one single set of laws to rule movement, one set to explain it, one law to to bring them all, and in the Newtonian Theory bind them.

Relativistic physics

Two key ideas:

  1. Laws of physics are the same for every observer
  2. The speed of light is constant

(Ignoring details about acceleration)

Part of what makes this physics philosophically interesting is that it defies our ordinary understanding of matter and thus has received a huge amount of attention in the media.
Basic idea: if the laws of physics always remain the same and the speed of light is constant, then space and time for two observers at different speeds is different.
(This is even weirder when acceleration and gravity come into play, but let's keep setting those issues aside.)
The idea that time and space are not the same regardless of frame of reference is deeply counterintuitive. Our intuition tends to favour the view that time and space are absolute.
Let us see this for time.
    A bit more detail:
  1. Special relativity (no details)
  2. General relativity (gravity and acc)
Special relativity (1905) was first proposed by Einstein and needs no special maths; general relativity (1915) requires a way of dealing with acceleration and gravity and accordingly requires a stronger mathematical apparatus.
Intuitive formulation
  1. Special relativity
    • If two frames of references differ only in their relative constante speed, physical phenomena behave the same.
    • The speed of light is always constant, regardless of frame of reference. C = 3·108 m/s (aprox)
  2. General relativity
    • Laws of physics are the same for every frame of reference.
    • The effects of gravity and acceleration are indistinguishable
One of the main consequences of general relativity is that we can see gravity as a result of the existence of mass in space-time.


Gravitational waves happen when big masses cease to exist. They've been recently meassured with LIGO.
The idea of space-time can be intuitively understood as a two dimensional plane where gravity can be seen as the result of distortions in it.

Quantum physics

  1. Facts
  2. Mathematical model
  3. Interpretation

Facts

Subatomic partices, on some occasions, behave like waves and, on other occasions, behave like particles.

Mathematical model

In order to account for the fact, a mathematical model of probability and waves is needed. The wave function is a representacion of the position, momentum or spin of a given particle. (In general, every meassurement in the system is given through a wave function.) Predictions in the system are the result of mathematical operations on wave functions.

Graphical representation of the wave function of the electrons in a hydrogen atom at different energy leves. (A brighter color represents a higher probability of finding an electron)

When meassured, the "cloud of possibilities" collapses into one point: the observed position of the electron.

(Compare this to the Rutherford model!)

Intepretation

The experimental results (facts) and the models (maths) do not however favour a specific interpretation. Many options are possible

Copenhague interpretation

A particles does not have a definite state before meassurement. When observed, the wave function collapses.

Wave function collapse: several Eigenstates possible reduce to one, ie, the observed one

Wave function: the mathematical descripcion of a system's quantum state.

Realist interpretation

Quantum theory is an incomplete theory. Einstein Podolsky Rosen (but more P than E and R): The wave function is incomplete: the reason why the values for a given property are not known is that information is missing. There are unknown hidden variables

Bell's theorem: hidden variables are inconsistent with the results predicted by the theory

Separation: The state of a particle system does not depende on the state of other particles when they are distant enough in space.
Completeness: if a certain physical property in fact holds for one partide at a given time, then the state function for the combined system at that time should yield probability one for finding that the property does hold.
Separation: The state of a particle system does not depend on the state of other particles when they are distant enough in space.
Completeness: if a certain physical property in fact holds for one particle at a given time, then the state function for the combined system at that time should yield probability one for finding that the property does hold.

Completeness and separation are incompatible!

Non-local interpretation: the system is complete but there is no separation. (This goes against the claim that nothing can travel faster than light)
Multiverse interpretation: there is no collapse. Several states of the probability are actual, just that in parallel universes.
Instrumentalist interpretation: Nevermind those questions; let's keep playing with the LHC!.

NOA: the natural ontological attitude

Philosophy of science is as usefull to scientists as ornithology is to birds.
—Richard Feynman
The attitude, to let science stand on its own and to view it without the support of philosophical isms, is what characterizes [the Natural Ontological Attitude]. (Fine 1986:9)
ehmmm..... not quite....
NOA: Our attitude towards science should be like our pre-reflective attitude towards the ordinary world. We accept the existence of objects like tables, chairs, clouds, trees and mountains, although we do not have a precise ways of defining them. They make up our world. NOA suggests: ditto for science.
But science has talked about ether, caloric, atoms, natural tendencies, quarks, spins, electrons, etc. How should we construe the ontological status of such entitites of the past and present science?
